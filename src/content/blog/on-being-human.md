There comes a peculiar age where life quietly stops asking whether you're capable and begins asking whether you can continue despite not knowing the answer. We spend years believing that effort and sincerity form a contract with the universe; that if we love deeply, work honestly, and remain kind, life will eventually respond in kind. Then reality interrupts, and we discover that meaning was never hidden inside outcomes. It was hidden inside the manner in which we carried ourselves while pursuing them.

Emotions are strange companions. They insist on being felt before they can ever be understood. We often mistake them for truth when, more often than not, they are simply witnesses to our unfinished stories. A heart that aches is not necessarily broken, just as a mind full of doubt is not necessarily lost. To be human is to repeatedly confuse temporary weather for permanent climate.

Perhaps maturity is not the disappearance of grief, fear, or longing, but the quiet realization that they need not dictate the direction of our lives. We can miss what once brought us peace without chasing it. We can mourn versions of ourselves that no longer exist while still making room for the person we are becoming.

The world celebrates certainty, yet almost every meaningful life is built upon unanswered questions. We search for purpose in achievements, in people, in recognition, only to discover that none of them can permanently answer the oldest question of all: Am I enough? That answer is never awarded by a title, a victory, or another person's affection. It is cultivated, often painfully, in the countless moments where we choose integrity over bitterness, curiosity over cynicism, and hope over resignation.

Perhaps life was never meant to be fully understood. Maybe its beauty lies precisely in its contradictions, in the questions that refuse to surrender to answers. We spend years chasing a definitive meaning, convinced that somewhere there exists a final explanation for our joys and our suffering. In that pursuit, we often begin to romanticize pain itself, mistaking endurance for purpose and wounds for identity. Yet perhaps the meaning we seek was never waiting to be discovered. Perhaps it is quietly created in the way we continue to love, to forgive, to remain curious, and to begin again despite never receiving complete certainty.

Maybe existence was never meant to be conquered. Maybe it was meant to be inhabited, with all its oddities, disappointments, fleeting joys, and impossible hopes. And perhaps the measure of a life is not how rarely we fall apart, but how gently we learn to gather ourselves again.

For in the end, we are not remembered for the certainty with which we walked, but for the courage with which we continued walking despite not seeing the road ahead.

The pain was so immense that night
my untamed heart longed to wrestle
with every vein that carried life within me.

It wished to spill from every pore of my being.
And somewhere, far away, in your courtyard,

it seemed as though every leaf,
washed in the sorrow of my blood,
began to appear wounded,
its moonlit beauty dimmed by grief.

Within the desolation of my body,
it was as if every aching thread of my existence
loosened itself,

and one after another
began announcing the preparations
for the departure of the caravan of longing.

Then, amidst the fading candles of memory,
there appeared,
for a fleeting moment,
the last trace of your tenderness.

The pain was so unbearable that I wished to pass beyond even it.
I tried...
but my heart refused to come to rest.

*From a celebrated Urdu poem by Faiz Ahmed Faiz*

—فا خر
